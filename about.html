About This Archive

A passion for history, preserved in metal — one coin at a time.

"Every coin in this collection is a survivor. It has outlasted the empire that minted it, the dynasty that fell, the hands that spent it. To hold one is to touch history in the most literal sense. This archive exists to share that experience with anyone who wishes to look."

The Collection

The Indian Numismatic is a personal collection of over 100 historical coins, assembled over many years with a focus on the Indian subcontinent. The collection spans more than 2,600 years of monetary history — from tiny Mauryan punch-marked silver pieces of the 4th century B.C. to the final issues of British India before independence in 1947.

Each coin has been carefully sourced, authenticated, graded, and documented. High-resolution photographs are taken under controlled lighting conditions to capture every detail of the obverse, reverse, and edge. The descriptive notes draw on published references including Standard Reference of Indian Coins (SRIC), Mitchiner's Oriental Coins, and Pridmore's Coins of the British Commonwealth.

Purpose & Philosophy

This website is built for education and the sharing of knowledge. No coins are offered for sale here. The goal is to make an excellent reference resource freely available to students, researchers, historians, and fellow enthusiasts — particularly those in India, where access to international numismatic literature can be limited.

Numismatics is often called the "hobby of kings" — but it need not be exclusive. A common copper paisa found in the soil of Karnataka carries as much historical significance as a gold mohur from the royal treasury. This archive aims to treat every coin with equal seriousness.

A Brief History of Indian Coinage

~600 B.C.

Punch-Marked Coins

The Mahajanapadas introduce silver punch-marked coins — India's earliest currency, and among the earliest in the world.

~150 B.C.

Indo-Greek Coinage

Following Alexander's campaigns, Greek rulers in Bactria and northwest India produce bilingual coins of extraordinary artistry — the finest portraiture of the ancient East.

320 – 550 A.D.

Gupta Gold Dinars

The Gupta Empire issues magnificent gold dinars depicting emperors in dynamic poses — hunting lions, playing the veena, performing yajnas.

1206 A.D.

Delhi Sultanate

Qutb-ud-din Aibak establishes the Delhi Sultanate and introduces Islamic coinage to north India — silver tankas with Kufic Arabic legends replace the old Hindu and Buddhist imagery.

1526 – 1858 A.D.

Mughal Empire

The Mughals create the most diverse and refined coinage in Indian history — from Akbar's square rupees to Jahangir's extraordinary portrait mohurs and zodiac coins.

1757 – 1947 A.D.

British India

The East India Company, then the Crown, introduces machine-struck coinage that standardises currency across the subcontinent for the first time — ending centuries of regional monetary tradition.

Every coin photographed under controlled lighting. Obverse, reverse, and edge detail captured where possible.
