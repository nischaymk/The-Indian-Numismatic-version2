Coins Through Time

India's numismatic story spans over 2,600 years. Each era produced coinage of distinct character, artistry, and political vision.

🏛️ 600 B.C. — 600 A.D.

Ancient India

The earliest coins of India — the silver punch-marked karshapanas of the Mauryan Empire — are among the world's first currencies. The era also encompasses the extraordinary art of the Gupta gold dinars and the unique bilingual coinage of the Indo-Greek kingdoms.

Coins of this period serve as primary historical documents. They preserve the names of rulers otherwise unknown from any other source, and their iconography captures the religious and political ideals of entire civilisations.

🌴 200 A.D. — 1800 A.D.

South Indian Kingdoms

The coinage of South India rivals that of any world civilisation in variety and beauty. The tiny gold fanams of Travancore and Cochin, the magnificent pagodas of Vijayanagara, and the punch-marked coins of the Satavahanas all form part of this remarkable tradition.

Iconography draws heavily from Vaishnavism and Shaivism — deities, conch shells, fish, and elephants appear as recurring symbols of dynastic identity.

☪️ 1206 A.D. — 1858 A.D.

Mughal & Sultanate

The Delhi Sultanate introduced true Islamic coinage to the subcontinent, and the Mughal emperors elevated coin-making to a high art. Akbar's square rupees, Jahangir's zodiac gold coins, and the calligraphic masterpieces of Aurangzeb represent numismatic achievements unmatched in Indian history.

Tipu Sultan's distinctive coinage — including the rare copper pieces from the Pattan mint illustrated on this site — bridges the Islamic and indigenous South Indian traditions.

👑 17th – 20th Century

Princely States

At their zenith, over 560 princely states issued their own coinage — from the silver rupees of Hyderabad (which rivalled British India issues in quality) to the crude copper coins of tiny hill states. Each is a unique historical artefact.

The variety is staggering: Kutch koris in Arabic script, Mewar gold mohurs, Baroda silver, and Travancore's distinctive conch-shell coins are all prized by collectors worldwide.

⚓ 1757 — 1947 A.D.

Colonial Era

British India's coinage is among the most methodically documented in the world. From the East India Company's early issues — often struck over Mughal coins — to the standardised Victorian and Edwardian rupees, these coins circulated across the entire subcontinent.

Key transitional pieces include the 1862 Victoria portrait series and the stunning proof issues struck for Indian rulers. Machine-struck precision meets a rich imperial iconography.
